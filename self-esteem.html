Self Esteem aka Rebecca Lucy Taylor released her new album Prioritise Pleasure a couple of weeks ago, which coincided with two of my lovely friends visiting me. At every opportunity that weekend, whether we were drinking wine and getting ready to go out or making breakfast in the morning - we had the new album on repeat. We listened to an interview with her on the radio as we made margaritas and talked about her pretty much non-stop. It got me wondering why Self Esteem has had such a huge impact on me and those around me.

Rotherham born Rebecca started out in the folksy, indie duo Slow Club before going solo with the bold and wonderful Self Esteem. Rebecca openly talks about the reasons she left Slow Club, namely that she was sick of being put in a box as the submissive, meek female. Rebecca has said that she was tired of shrinking herself and compromising who she really was, so learnt to say a big fuck it to everything she thought she was supposed to be doing and set out on a mission to shed her people pleasing tendencies. Rebecca talks about how going solo with Self Esteem has helped her to truly express herself and break free from seeking approval. On her social media, she often shares messages from men who tell her that they much preferred her in Slow Club, which I think says it all really. People don’t like to see confident, self-assured women

Self Esteem writes songs that we can all relate to. Listening to her music feels like a drunken conversation with your best mate. She reminds me of parts of myself and my friends and all the great people that I know. Her new album is of the same calibre as her first, and I have had it on repeat ever since it came out. Has there ever been a better opening line to a song than ‘sexting you at the mental health talk seems counterproductive’? I think not. One of the songs from her new album has a fantastic part that is completely made up of comments that men have said to her throughout her life, including a Slow Club tour manager saying to her ‘all you need to do darling is fit in that little dress of yours’. Disgusting, but not surprising. Another of her new songs has a sound clip from some work she did at the National Youth Theatre, where a group of young women are talking about how if they are approached by a group of men they will bark like dogs – ‘as there is nothing that terrifies a man more than a woman who appears completely deranged’ which I think is just great and very relatable.

The genre of Self Esteem has also been interesting to me. When I was about 18, I mostly listening to indie music made by men. I thought that liking pop music was a bit embarrassing and basic, and I realise now that this is because I had internalised this view that a fun media form dominated by women was subpar. It’s the same sort of vibe as when people mock women for getting a boomerang of their cocktails, as if being a woman and having fun with your friends is something to be ashamed of. As I’ve gotten older, I’ve realised that pop is really bloody great. There’s this totally wrong idea that pop music is superficial and one dimensional, and Self Esteem breaks all those stereotypes through showing us that pop can be exciting and experimental whilst also making some big important, comments on the patriarchy. Her songs are sometimes upbeat and fun and sometimes slow with poignant lyrics and big strings. Often singing about relationships, I appreciate the fact that whilst she ardently sings about toxic, awful exes, she also recognises the part that she has played in causing harm to those she loved, holding a mirror up to us all as an example of accountability.

My love for Self Esteem is wrapped up in so many memories. I Do This All The Time reminds me of being on a road trip with my friend, both of us singing it at the top of our lungs and having that goosebumpy, euphoric feeling. In Time reminds me of seeing her live in Brixton and the whole crowd shouting along to the line SO I’M GONNA GET DRUNK AND SLAG YOU OFF THEN I’M GONNA GO HOME AND EAT MY FEELINGS - a sense of us all having been there and knowing exactly how that feels. Steady I Stand reminds me of a strength I mustered during a particularly rocky time a couple of years back, where I would listen to that song on repeat and remind myself that I still somehow had my feet firmly on the ground. The Best reminds me of living with my amazing pal when I had just moved to London and we would play it in the kitchen whilst we cooked dinner. Although her new album has just come out, I feel a real sense of nostalgia and the songs remind me of past experiences and feelings I’ve had. I’ve got a long way to go to reach the levels of authenticity and self-acceptance that Self Esteem has, but I am sure that she is helping me along my way.
